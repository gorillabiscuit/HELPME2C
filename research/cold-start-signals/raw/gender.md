# Signal: Gender

**Currently collected:** No
**Recommended tier:** DON'T-ASK

## What it is

A self-reported gender field at signup, typically taking values along the spectrum {woman, man, non-binary, prefer-to-self-describe, prefer-not-to-say}. In a privacy-by-default product like HelpME2C the question would be optional; in less considered designs it ships as a required dropdown with two values. The signal is conceptually distinct from biological sex (which would be Art 9 special-category territory) and from sexual orientation (always Art 9). For HelpME2C it would only ever exist as a soft taste-modulating prior in cold-start scoring.

## Predictive value (literature / industry)

The honest answer is: **gender is a weak, biased, and increasingly avoided proxy for taste in entertainment recsys.** The literature has moved in roughly one direction over the past five years — from "demographic filtering improves cold start" to "demographic filtering encodes stereotype and produces measurably worse experiences for some user groups, especially when used as a substitute for real preference signal."

The pro-side evidence exists but is thin. Demographic-integrated collaborative filtering on the MovieLens 1M dataset shows accuracy improvements (one paper reports 17.1% RMSE / 14.4% MAE improvement over KNN-Basic using gender + age + occupation + zip code) ([Enhancing Movie Recommendations: A Demographic-Integrated Cosine-KNN Collaborative Filtering Approach, 2024](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/385213564_Enhancing_Movie_Recommendations_A_Demographic-Integrated_Cosine-KNN_Collaborative_Filtering_Approach)). But notably, **gender is bundled with three other features** in those gains, and the broader literature is explicit that gender alone is a poor predictor: "Features such as age, gender, and occupation of users aren't very sophisticated predictors of users' preferences for movies, because there isn't very high correlation between demographic information and user's likeness for a movie" ([Predicting user demographics based on interest analysis in movie dataset, Multimedia Tools and Applications, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11042-024-18422-6)).

The critique-side evidence is much stronger. The Spotify literature is now extensive: collaborative filtering recommenders reproduce dataset bias such that female artists are pushed down to rank 6–7 on average while male artists hold rank 1 ([Ferraro et al., "Break the Loop: Gender Imbalance in Music Recommenders," 2021](https://christinebauer.eu/publications/ferraro-2021-break-the-loop/ferraro-2021-break-the-loop.pdf); [Music Tomorrow analysis](https://www.music-tomorrow.com/blog/fairness-and-diversity-in-music-recommendation-algorithms)). Female users receive measurably less accurate recommendations than male users in Spotify-style systems ([Investigating gender fairness of recommendation algorithms in the music domain, ScienceDirect 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0306457321001540)). This is the "you might like Madonna because you're female" failure mode operationalised: when you feed gender into the scoring function, you don't get better recommendations — you get the algorithm's prior about what a woman should like, which is bigger and louder than the user's actual signal.

Most damningly for our case: a January 2025 paper, "A Closer Look on Gender Stereotypes in Movie Recommender Systems and Their Implications with Privacy" ([arXiv:2501.04420](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.04420)), shows that an attacker can infer gender from movie preferences with high accuracy by exploiting genre stereotypes — and that recommender systems trained with gender features *encode and amplify those stereotypes*. Combined with [BlurMe (2012)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/254464310_BlurMe_inferring_and_obfuscating_user_gender_based_on_ratings)'s 80%+ gender-inference accuracy from ratings alone, this means: (a) we don't need to ask, the rating signal already carries gender as a latent variable, and (b) explicitly conditioning on gender hard-codes stereotype into the output.

For HelpME2C specifically — group recommendations across a couch co-watcher pair, anime fan with non-anime partner, etc. — gender of the partner adds nothing that "what shows do they actually like / hate" doesn't already capture better. The household-composition signal (asked separately) provides the group structure; preference data provides taste. Inserting gender between those two adds no predictive value but adds a stereotype-encoding pathway. The Pinterest engineering writeup of their inclusive-gender redesign explicitly noted that *the added options did not adversely impact metrics* — but it also implies gender is not a load-bearing prediction signal for them either ([Pinterest Engineering, Building expanded gender options on signup](https://medium.com/pinterest-engineering/building-expanded-gender-options-on-signup-19e010151d03)).

## GDPR / consent cost

Gender is **not** a special category under Art 9 — Art 9 covers "racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade-union membership, genetic data, biometric data, data concerning health or a natural person's sex life or sexual orientation" ([Art. 9 GDPR](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-9-gdpr/)). The Baker McKenzie analysis is explicit that "GDPR does not recognise gender or sex data as a special category of personal data" ([The protection of gender identity under the GDPR, Baker McKenzie](https://connectontech.bakermckenzie.com/the-protection-of-gender-identity-under-the-gdpr-2/)). So gender is processed under Art 6 like any other personal data.

But "not Art 9" is not "freely collectible." Three constraints still bite. First, Art 5(1)(c) data minimisation: "adequate, relevant and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed." If the signal doesn't materially improve recs (and the literature above says it doesn't), processing it fails the necessity test ([ICO Data Minimisation](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/data-protection-principles/a-guide-to-the-data-protection-principles/data-minimisation/)). Second, CNIL has emphasised proportionality measures for demographic data even outside Art 9: "to guarantee that its processing is proportionate, the controller may implement measures such as pseudonymisation, ensuring the absence of sensitive data, defining selection criteria to limit the collection to the relevant and necessary data" ([CNIL, Ensuring the lawfulness of data processing](https://www.cnil.fr/en/ensuring-lawfulness-data-processing-legal-basis)). Third, gender identity edge cases can spill into Art 9 territory: data revealing that someone is transgender is widely treated as touching "data concerning sex life" or as inherently sensitive, even if "gender" as a field is not ([Defining 'sensitive data': How do different states treat questions of gender and sexuality?, Lexology](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=6b984353-d151-4011-b98b-59ce10eaa360)).

There's no cookie-banner trigger from collecting gender, but ADR-0012's framing of HelpME2C as a privacy-by-default product means the bar is "materially improves rec quality," and gender does not clear that bar.

## UX / drop-off cost

Gender is repeatedly named by Baymard as one of the fields that materially raises drop-off when required: "privacy concerns regarding collection of personal data can be alleviated by making requests for sensitive information such as phone number, gender, and birth date optional, or by explicitly explaining why the information is needed" ([Baymard, 19 Ways to Simplify Sign-Up](https://baymard.com/blog/simplifying-sign-up)). The general form-length literature says every extra field costs 8–50% conversion, and 81% of mobile users abandon forms perceived as too long ([Build Grow Scale, 81% mobile drop-off](https://buildgrowscale.com/reduce-form-abandonment)). Optional and explained is a partial mitigation but not a free pass.

Inclusive-gender design adds further cost in implementation but not necessarily in drop-off. Pinterest's iteration shows the pattern works ([Pinterest Engineering blog](https://medium.com/pinterest-engineering/building-expanded-gender-options-on-signup-19e010151d03)) — radio buttons + custom text + tooltip explaining why — but it also illustrates how much design and i18n work it costs to do correctly (their "custom" label didn't fit in German or French). For a 4/5-quality-bar-on-10-testers MVP that's a lot of UX surface for a signal we shouldn't be reading. Item-nonresponse research on gender questions is reassuring on a separate dimension: only ~0.1% of respondents stop the survey entirely at the gender field when "prefer not to say" is offered ([Survey Practice, "I Prefer Not to Respond" article](https://www.surveypractice.org/article/143682-i-prefer-not-to-respond-survey-responses-red-flag-or-red-herring)) — so the cost is less catastrophic abandonment, more incremental friction and trust erosion.

NN/G's EAS framework is the cleanest counsel here: **Eliminate first**, before automating or simplifying ([NN/G, EAS Framework](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/eas-framework-simplify-forms/)). Eliminate is the move when there's no signal to capture.

## Path into scoring

There is no clean deterministic path into `packages/ml/src/recommendation.ts`. The current scorer reads anchor picks + rated titles, both of which are higher-signal-per-bit than a demographic prior. A naive use ("re-weight female-coded genres for women") is exactly the stereotype-encoding failure mode the critique literature warns against and would degrade rec quality, not improve it. A defensible use (debiasing — explicitly *under*-correcting for stereotype) requires more sophistication than rule-based Phase 1A allows. The honest answer: any rule-based use we'd write today is a worse predictor than what we already have, and any responsible use of gender data needs ML infrastructure we've explicitly ruled out for Phase 1A. That should tank the tier.

## Tier justification

**Recommended: DON'T-ASK.**

The constraints box sets the bar at "materially improves rec quality, not might be useful." Gender doesn't clear that bar. The signal is weak (best-case ~17% RMSE improvement is bundled with three other features, not gender alone); the failure mode is well-documented (Spotify-style stereotype encoding, female users getting less accurate recs); the data is already latent in ratings (BlurMe shows 80%+ inference accuracy without asking, which means we *get* the signal whether we want to or not — and we should be obfuscating it, not amplifying it); and Phase 1A's rule-based scorer has no responsible path to use it without hard-coding stereotype.

The privacy posture compounds: not Art 9 but proximate to it (gender identity edge cases tip into special-category territory), and asking it puts HelpME2C on the wrong side of the data-minimisation / necessity test. For the four target archetypes (couch co-watcher, anime fan with non-anime partner, casual watcher, power tracker), gender doesn't define the archetype — household composition and viewing intent do, and those are asked elsewhere. Skipping gender is one fewer field, one less stereotype-encoding pathway, one less Art 5(1)(c) exposure, and zero loss in rec quality. The default of DON'T-ASK from the constraints box is the right answer.
