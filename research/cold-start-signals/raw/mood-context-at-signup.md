# Signal: Mood / context at signup

**Currently collected:** No
**Recommended tier:** DON'T-ASK

## What it is

A one-time onboarding capture of the user's preferred *mood* or *tonal context* for what they want to watch — typically expressed via sliders (light ↔ dark, slow ↔ fast, comfort ↔ challenge) or chip selectors ("escapist", "thought-provoking", "cosy"). The signal is set once during signup and either persists as a stable filter on subsequent recommendation requests, or is treated as the seed for a more permanent "taste mood profile". This is distinct from *mood at request time*, which is a per-session prompt ("what are you in the mood for tonight?") that is re-asked on each visit. The brief specifically scopes this signal to **mood at signup** — the one-time, persistent variant.

## Predictive value (literature / industry)

The strongest empirical critique of one-time mood capture comes from the session-based recsys literature, which has spent a decade documenting that mood and intent are **session-scoped**, not user-scoped. A canonical survey notes that "the sequence of songs listened to may actually matter, reflecting the mood and interests of the user in real time" and that "a user may be in the mood to listen to the 'most popular' songs, but at other times they are looking to discover something new" — i.e. the same user has *different* mood states across sessions and modelling them as a static profile attribute is a misspecification ([arXiv — A Survey on Session-based Recommender Systems](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1902.04864); [Springer — Empirical analysis of session-based recommendation algorithms](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11257-020-09277-1)). A practitioner-side framing makes the same point bluntly: "Most streaming algorithms still optimize for who you are in general, not what you need right now, and closing this gap requires… inferring session intent and ephemeral state (mood, time budget, social context), then re-ranking accordingly" ([Medium — Mood & Intent Awareness in Video Recommendations](https://medium.com/@udilumnitz/mood-intent-awareness-in-video-recommendations-from-static-taste-to-right-now-relevance-cc3360c0dd90)). Both arguments converge on the same conclusion: mood signal is valuable *if* collected at request time, and structurally weak if collected at signup.

Spotify is the most-cited industry case for mood-aware recommendation and is often misread as evidence for *asking* about mood. It is not. Spotify's mood inference is overwhelmingly *implicit*: the platform "continuously ingests user behaviour and environmental signals to deliver in-the-moment recommendations optimized for mood, activity, and time of day", and its public patents describe inferring mood from "speech, ambient cues, and usage patterns" rather than asking ([Medium — Behind the Playlist: Analyzing Spotify's Recommendation System](https://medium.com/@myliemudaliyar/behind-the-playlist-analyzing-spotifys-recommendation-system-5044a13f5ccf); [TechAhead — How Spotify Recommendation System Works](https://www.techaheadcorp.com/blog/spotify-recommendation-system/)). Where explicit mood-asking has been studied, the results are equivocal at best: a 2025 CHI-adjacent paper on emotion-regulation prompts in music apps reports user concerns about acceptability and disclosure rather than clear quality wins ([ACM DL — Would You Tell Spotify How You're Feeling?](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3726986.3726998)). The pattern is consistent: mood is a powerful *runtime* feature and a weak *registration-time* feature.

Mubi is the streaming product most often cited as a mood/curation success, and again the architecture argues *against* asking. Mubi's editorial approach — a rotating set of 30 hand-picked films and the Notebook editorial layer — replaces user-input mood with *curatorial* mood signalling ([Cardiff University — Beyond Netflix and Amazon: MUBI and the Curation of On-Demand Film](https://orca.cardiff.ac.uk/id/eprint/117638/1/Beyond%20Netflix%20and%20Amazon_.pdf); [Wikipedia — MUBI (streaming service)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mubi_(streaming_service))). The product imports mood through editorial collections like "Cosy Sunday Afternoon", not through user self-report. Letterboxd-adjacent recommender experiments and third-party tools similarly derive mood from review-emotion clustering and tag aggregation, not signup forms ([Nanocrowd — Letterboxd Mood Collections](https://nanocrowd.com/nanocrowd-letterboxd/); [Medium — Mood-Based Movie Recommendation System](https://medium.com/@indubarnwal752/mood-based-movie-recommendation-system-part-1-0466bdf059a2)). The dominant industry pattern for mood-as-product is curatorial or implicit, not registration-time questionnaire.

The cold-start case-for-asking exists but is narrow. The CARS (Context-Aware Recommender Systems) lineage at RecSys has consistently shown that explicit context helps *more* in cold-start than in steady state — but the same literature emphasises *context at decision time* (location, time-of-day, companion, device), not a one-shot static prior ([ResearchGate — Context-Aware Recommender Systems in the Music Domain: A Systematic Literature Review](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/352792887_Context-Aware_Recommender_Systems_in_the_Music_Domain_A_Systematic_Literature_Review); [Frontiers — An overview of video recommender systems](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/big-data/articles/10.3389/fdata.2023.1281614/full)). The signal that *does* move cold-start outcomes is, per the survey work, "fine-grained user-item context" — i.e. the very thing a one-time signup mood does *not* provide.

## GDPR / consent cost

Mood data is personal data but at the low-sensitivity end. It does not reveal health or special-category attributes unless the question is phrased to probe psychological state (e.g. "are you depressed today?" — which would itself be a §3-banned question). The lawful basis is clean (Article 6(1)(a) consent or 6(1)(f) legitimate interest). The harder issue is **data minimisation** (Article 5(1)(c)): the ICO and EDPB framing requires that the data be "limited to what is necessary" and the EDPB necessity test asks whether the purpose "cannot reasonably be achieved just as effectively by other means less restrictive" ([ICO — Principle (c): Data minimisation](https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/data-protection-principles/a-guide-to-the-data-protection-principles/data-minimisation/); [EDPB — Guidelines 1/2024 on Legitimate Interest](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2024-10/edpb_guidelines_202401_legitimateinterest_en.pdf)).

This is where mood-at-signup runs into trouble specifically as a *signup* signal. The evidence (§Predictive value) is that the genuine product win is mood-at-request-time. If the same UX outcome can be achieved by asking the user "what are you in the mood for tonight?" *when they open the app to find something*, the EDPB necessity test points toward not asking at signup at all — the less intrusive alternative exists and is also the better-performing one. CLAUDE.md §4 stop-and-ask on "any new persisted user preference" applies, and the answer to that stop-and-ask is: don't persist this; ask it ephemerally if at all.

## UX / drop-off cost

Mood capture is the *highest-cost* widget per question on the menu. Sliders demand calibration ("what does '7 out of 10 dark' even mean?"), chip selectors demand multi-select decisions, and either widget invites the very decision-fatigue pattern that bites past 3–4 simultaneous choices ([SaaSFactor — Why Users Drop Off During Onboarding](https://www.saasfactor.co/blogs/why-users-drop-off-during-onboarding-and-how-to-fix-it); [Baymard — Account Configuration & Onboarding](https://baymard.com/ecommerce-design-examples/account-configuration-and-onboarding)). Baymard's 3–5% completion drop per added field compounds against a question whose product value is weak in the first place. NN/G's "skip it when possible" stance applies forcefully here ([NN/G — Onboarding: Skip it When Possible](https://www.nngroup.com/videos/onboarding-skip-it-when-possible/)).

There is also a narrative cost: mood questions create an implicit promise ("the recs will reflect this mood") that the rule-based 1A engine cannot reliably fulfil. A user who says "dark and slow" at signup and then sees recommendations driven by anchor picks and tag overlap will perceive the mood input as cosmetic, eroding trust in the onboarding flow itself.

## Path into scoring

In `packages/ml/src/recommendation.ts` today, mood would have to land as either a post-hoc filter on candidate sets or a small additive boost on tag overlaps that match curated "mood tag" buckets (e.g. tags clustered as "dark"). Both are mechanically implementable but require a meaningful side-investment: a tag-to-mood mapping that is either editorially curated (expensive to maintain) or LLM-derived (which would itself need an ADR per §4). The deterministic path exists; the cost-to-value ratio is poor for 1A — especially compared to household composition, which plugs into infrastructure that *already exists* (the `family-with-constraint` aggregation in `packages/ml/src/eval/fixtures.ts`).

If the signal is needed, the cheaper and better-performing implementation is mood-at-request-time as a transient session filter, queried via a "tonight I want…" affordance on the recs surface rather than at signup.

## Tier justification

**Recommended: DON'T-ASK.**

The constraints box sets the bar at "materially improves rec quality, not might be useful" and the evidence (1) shows that mood is structurally a session-scoped signal that decays fast as a static prior, (2) shows that the streaming products most associated with mood-driven recs (Spotify, Mubi) collect it *implicitly* or *editorially*, not via signup forms, and (3) shows that any genuine win lives at request-time, where the EDPB data-minimisation necessity test pushes the same direction. The 1A scoring path requires a side-investment (mood-tag mapping) for a signal whose value is weakest in the form being proposed. Confirming the existing deferral is the right call — and the framing should be sharpened from "deferred to 1B" to "ask at request-time, never at signup", which avoids re-litigating this in 1B with the same evidence.
