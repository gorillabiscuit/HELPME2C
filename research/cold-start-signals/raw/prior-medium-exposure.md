# Signal: Prior medium exposure ("Have you watched anime before?")

**Currently collected:** No
**Recommended tier:** NICE-TO-HAVE (binary "any anime exposure?" toggle only, with skip — not a question worth screen real estate on its own; route into a side affordance if it appears at all)

## What it is

A binary onboarding question asking whether the user has ever meaningfully watched a medium that HelpME2C cross-recommends from (in Phase 1A: anime, since live-action TV is the cultural default for most Western users). The intent is to detect newcomers to the medium so HelpME2C can serve a curated *starter set* (e.g. gateway anime: *Fullmetal Alchemist: Brotherhood*, *Cowboy Bebop*, *Death Note*, *Demon Slayer*) rather than ranking deeper cuts that assume genre fluency. This is **distinct from "medium balance preference"** (already researched, landed DON'T-ASK): balance is "what % of your viewing is anime?" — a continuous mix preference. Exposure is "have you tried it *at all*?" — a one-bit question about whether the user can parse anime as a genre at all. A user who answers "no" to balance might still have watched *Spirited Away* in childhood; a user who answers "no" to exposure has never seen anything animated from Japan and likely needs the system to teach them what the medium is before recommending niche titles.

## Predictive value (literature / industry)

The research literature broadly favours *deriving* taste signals from rated items rather than asking direct preference questions, because direct questions tend to be answered with stated rather than revealed preferences. Rashid et al.'s foundational study on cold-start preference elicitation tested popularity-based, entropy-based, and balanced strategies for choosing which 20 items to show new users, and concluded that *direct rating of items* produced more accurate user models than meta-questions ([Rashid et al., "Getting to Know You", IUI 2002](https://cs.fit.edu/~pkc/apweb/related/rashid-iui02.pdf)). The 2018 ACM RecSys paper on "Preference elicitation as an optimization problem" reaches the same conclusion in stronger terms: optimal cold-start onboarding asks the *minimum, most diverse* set of questions, and an extra meta-screening question is only worth it if it provably reduces the number of follow-up rating questions required ([Sepliarskaia et al., RecSys 2018](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3240323.3240352)).

That said, there is an empirically validated counter-argument specifically for *medium familiarity*. The "Genre Familiarity Correlation-Based Recommender Algorithm" line of work explicitly uses a familiarity prior per genre — derived from how exposed the user has been to that genre — and reports better cold-start performance than baseline collaborative filtering ([Genre Familiarity Correlation-Based Recommender Algorithm for New User Cold Start Problem, ScienceDirect 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S1548365721000182)). The reason this works is that genres are a categorical signal that is hard to infer from a small picks list when the user only picks within a few genres; explicitly asking unlocks recommendations *outside* the picked set. Anime in particular is a medium where being a "newcomer" is a real, distinct mode — the community publishes whole "gateway anime" canons exactly because the on-ramp matters ([CBR: 20 Best Starter Anime Series For Beginners](https://www.cbr.com/anime-for-beginners-newcomers/), [Netflix Tudum: Best Popular Beginner Anime](https://www.netflix.com/tudum/articles/anime-for-beginners), [Crunchyroll: 10 Best Anime Shows for Beginners](https://www.tvinsider.com/list/best-anime-shows-for-beginners-crunchyroll/)).

Industry behaviour is informative. Crunchyroll, Netflix, and AniList do NOT ask "are you new to anime?" at signup ([Crunchyroll Getting Started](https://help.crunchyroll.com/hc/en-us/articles/22728708616852-Getting-started-on-Crunchyroll-website), [Netflix Help: How Recommendations Work](https://help.netflix.com/en/node/100639)). Netflix's only onboarding signal is "choose a few titles you like" — Netflix explicitly relies on revealed preference and explicitly defaults to popular/diverse titles when the user skips ([Netflix Help Center: How Netflix's Recommendations System Works](https://help.netflix.com/en/node/100639)). Crunchyroll's onboarding goes straight to popular curated lists, leaving the "starter pack" affordance to *content surfaces* (recommendation rows like "Best for Beginners") rather than onboarding questions. This is a strong industry signal that asking "have you watched anime?" is over-engineered for the value delivered: if a user has anime in their picks, you know they have exposure; if they don't, you can serve a "Anime to start with" row inside the app and let curiosity convert.

The most useful framing from this literature for HelpME2C: the anchor picks already reveal exposure with high accuracy (a user who picks zero anime titles among their 5–6 anchors is almost certainly a non-watcher or an indifferent one), and the marginal value of an additional yes/no question is low *unless* the system would otherwise show that user nothing but deep-cut anime. For HelpME2C in Phase 1A, where scoring is rule-based and the recommendation surface can already include a "Anime starter pack" row triggered by *zero anime anchor picks*, the explicit question is largely redundant.

## GDPR / consent cost

Low-to-medium. "Have you watched anime?" is not special-category data under GDPR Article 9 and not directly identifying. However, it is a personal preference / behavioural signal that creates a profile facet, and the GDPR floor for HelpME2C is opt-in with a clear "why we're asking" affordance (per ADR-0012). Adding the question commits the codebase to documenting purpose ("to give you a starter list rather than deep recommendations") and to deleting that signal as part of `/api/account/delete`. The consent cost is not zero — every signal collected is a column on a Subject Access Request and a row to nuke on deletion — but it is materially lower than asking for, say, household composition.

A subtler consent cost is *implication*. Asking the question implies that HelpME2C will *behave differently* for newcomers — that we have a separate cold-start regime. That is a promise we should keep, otherwise the question is dishonest data collection. If we ask and then don't actually serve a different starter row, we've collected data we don't use, which is a soft GDPR data-minimisation violation.

## UX / drop-off cost

Each additional onboarding question is measurable friction. Industry data: 3-field forms convert at 25% vs. 7-field forms at 12% ([Foundry CRO: Form Conversion Rate Benchmarks 2026](https://foundrycro.com/blog/form-conversion-rate-benchmarks-2026/)), and every additional form field reduces completion by 3–5% ([Baymard Institute, via SaaSFactor](https://www.saasfactor.co/blogs/what-steps-should-your-signup-and-onboarding-include-to-reduce-drop-off)). The HelpME2C onboarding already includes anchor picks (the high-value primary signal) and any additional question competes with screen real estate and motivation.

The drop-off cost is partially mitigated if the question is a *single tap* — a binary toggle answerable in under two seconds — and if it lives on a screen the user is already on. The cost is much higher if it requires its own step. Pinterest's onboarding makes the cost concrete: their model is "pick 5 topics" presented as a single tile grid, and they explicitly do not stack a "are you new to Pinterest?" pre-question because the topic selection itself surfaces newcomers (someone with no clear five-topic spread gets a popular default) ([Appcues: How Pinterest perfected user onboarding](https://www.appcues.com/blog/casey-winters-pinterest-user-onboarding)). TikTok takes the same shape: interest categories first, no meta-questions about experience level ([Appcues: TikTok's onboarding](https://goodux.appcues.com/blog/tiktok-user-onboarding)).

## Path into scoring

If asked, the signal would route into `packages/ml/src/recommendation.ts` as a `userExposure.anime: 'none' | 'some' | 'fluent'` field, derivable as a multiplier on the candidate pool. Concretely: when `userExposure.anime === 'none'`, the engine would (a) restrict the candidate pool to a hardcoded ~25-title gateway list curated by a human, (b) skip the genre-overlap scoring component for anime candidates (since "overlap" with zero anime anchors is undefined), and (c) prefer dub-available, complete-series, sub-15-episode titles. Without the explicit signal, the same behaviour can be triggered by `anchorPicks.filter(p => p.medium === 'anime').length === 0` — which is the same fact, derived rather than asked.

## Tier justification

**Recommended: NICE-TO-HAVE.**

The constraint box says "materially improves rec quality" is the bar, not "might be useful". The literature and industry practice both support that anchor picks already encode exposure with sufficient fidelity for Phase 1A's rule-based scoring. The differentiated experience for anime-newcomers (a gateway starter pack) can be delivered as a *content surface*, not a *signup signal* — triggered by zero-anime anchors rather than by an explicit question. The genre-familiarity literature does establish that explicit exposure signals improve cold-start performance, but those gains are most pronounced for collaborative filtering at scale, not for rule-based scoring against a Phase 1A user base. Given GDPR's privacy-by-default floor and Baymard's 3–5% drop-off per extra field, the cost of asking exceeds the marginal benefit. Promote to SHOULD-ASK only if user testing reveals that anime-newcomers are getting recommendations they cannot parse — at which point a single-tap "I'm new to anime, show me starters" toggle on the *results page* (not the onboarding flow) is the right shape.
